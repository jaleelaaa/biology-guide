    Introduction

    When you look around, you see both living and non-living things. What makes an organism living? The answer is the presence of the basic unit of life ‚Äì the cell in all living organisms.

    All organisms are composed of cells. Some are composed of a single cell and are called unicellular organisms (like Amoeba, bacteria), while others, like us, composed of many cells, are called multicellular organisms.

    1. What is a Cell?

    1.1 Definition

    Cell: The fundamental structural and functional unit of all living organisms. It is the smallest unit of life that can function independently and perform all the necessary functions of life.

    Why Cell is Called the Unit of Life:

    1. Smallest Independent Unit: Unicellular organisms like Amoeba can perform all life processes (digestion, reproduction, excretion, growth) with just one cell
    2. Organelles Cannot Exist Independently: Only complete cells can survive and function independently
    3. Structural Unit: All organisms are made of cells
    4. Functional Unit: All life processes occur within cells

    Key Point: Anything less than a complete structure of a cell does not ensure independent living. Hence, cell is the fundamental structural and functional unit of all living organisms.

    2.2 Cell Theory

    Formulated by: Matthias Schleiden (1838) and Theodor Schwann (1839)
    Modified by: Rudolf Virchow (1855)

    The Three Postulates of Cell Theory:

    1. All living organisms are composed of cells and products of cells

      • Every living thing, whether bacteria or human, is made up of one or more cells

    2. The cell is the basic unit of life

      • Cells are the smallest units that can perform all life functions independently

    3. All cells arise from pre-existing cells

      • New cells are formed only by division of existing cells (added by Virchow)
      • Cells cannot form de novo from non-living materials

    Important: Rudolf Virchow's contribution (Omnis cellula-e cellula - all cells from cells) was crucial in completing the cell theory.

    Common Mistake: Don't say prokaryotes have "no nucleus" - they have genetic material, just not enclosed in a nuclear membrane. Say "no well-defined nucleus" or "no membrane-bound nucleus."

    3. Overview of Cell Structure

    3.1 Basic Cell Structure

    All cells have certain common features:

    Universal Components (Found in ALL cells - both prokaryotic and eukaryotic):

    1. Cell Membrane (Plasma Membrane): Outer boundary separating cell from environment
    2. Cytoplasm: Semi-fluid matrix where cellular activities occur
    3. Genetic Material (DNA): Stores hereditary information
    4. Ribosomes: Site of protein synthesis

    Additional Components (vary between cell types):

    • Cell Wall: Present in plants, fungi, bacteria; absent in animals
    • Nucleus: Well-defined in eukaryotes; absent in prokaryotes
    • Membrane-bound Organelles: Present in eukaryotes; absent in prokaryotes

    3.2 Cell Size and Shape

    Size Range:

    • Smallest: Mycoplasma (0.3 Œºm) - smallest cells, wall-less prokaryotes
    • Bacteria: 3-5 Œºm (e.g., E. coli)
    • Human RBC: ~7 Œºm diameter
    • Typical Eukaryotic Cell: 10-100 Œºm
    • Largest Single Cell: Ostrich egg

    Important: Œºm (micrometer) = 1/1000 mm. So 1 mm = 1000 Œºm.

    Shape Variation:

    Cells vary greatly in shape according to their function:

    Cell Type Shape Function/Reason
    Human RBC Biconcave disc Increases surface area for oxygen transport; can squeeze through capillaries
    White Blood Cells Amoeboid (irregular) Can change shape to squeeze out of blood vessels to reach infection sites
    Nerve Cells Branched with long tail Transfer information quickly over long distances
    Muscle Cells Elongated, spindle-shaped Facilitate contraction and movement
    Mesophyll Cells Round/Oval with chloroplasts Optimized for photosynthesis
    Tracheids Tubular, elongated Facilitate water transport against gravity; dead at maturity
    Columnar Epithelial Column-shaped Protection, secretion, absorption in intestines

    Key Concept: Form follows function - The shape of a cell is related to the function it performs.

    4. Types of Cells: Prokaryotic and Eukaryotic

    4.1 Classification Based on Nuclear Organization

    Prokaryotic Cells (pro = primitive, karyon = nucleus):

    • Cells whose DNA is NOT bound by a nuclear membrane
    • Primitive, simple cells
    • No membrane-bound organelles

    Eukaryotic Cells (eu = true, karyon = nucleus):

    • Cells with a well-defined membrane-bound nucleus
    • Advanced, complex cells
    • Membrane-bound organelles present

    5. Prokaryotic Cells

    5.1 General Characteristics

    Examples: Bacteria, Blue-green algae (Cyanobacteria), Mycoplasma, PPLO (Pleuro Pneumonia Like Organisms)

    Key Features:

    1. No true nucleus: Genetic material (DNA) in nucleoid region, not enclosed by nuclear membrane
    2. No membrane-bound organelles: No mitochondria, ER, Golgi apparatus, lysosomes, chloroplasts
    3. Smaller size: Generally 1-10 Œºm (smaller than eukaryotes)
    4. 70S ribosomes: Only ribosome type present
    5. Cell wall: Present (except Mycoplasma which is wall-less)
    6. Cell division: By binary fission (simple splitting)
    7. DNA: Single circular chromosome + plasmids (small circular DNA)
    8. Rapid multiplication: Divide more quickly than eukaryotic cells

    5.2 Bacterial Shapes

    Bacteria exhibit four basic shapes:

    Shape Name Description Example
    Coccus (plural: cocci) Spherical/Round Staphylococcus
    Bacillus (plural: bacilli) Rod-shaped E. coli, Lactobacillus
    Spirillum (plural: spirilla) Spiral-shaped Spirillum
    Vibrio (plural: vibrios) Comma-shaped Vibrio cholerae

    Memory Tip - CBSV:

    • Coccus - Circular/Spherical
    • Bacillus - Bat (Rod-shaped like a baseball bat)
    • Spirillum - Spiral
    • Vibrio - Vibrating comma

    5.3 Prokaryotic Cell Structure

    A. Cell Envelope (Three-layered protective covering)

    1. Glycocalyx (Outermost Layer)

    • Composition: Polysaccharides
    • Two Types:
      • Capsule: Thick, tightly bound, hard to remove - provides protection
      • Slime Layer/Sheath: Thin, loosely bound, easy to remove

    2. Cell Wall (Middle Layer)

    • Composition: Peptidoglycan (in bacteria)
    • Exception: Mycoplasma lacks cell wall (wall-less prokaryote)
    • Functions:
      • Determines cell shape
      • Provides structural and mechanical support
      • Prevents bursting or collapsing of cell

    Gram Staining (Classification based on cell wall):

    Developed by Christian Gram, a staining procedure to classify bacteria:

    Feature Gram Positive (+) Gram Negative (-)
    Stain Retention Retains Gram stain (blue) Loses stain (appears red/pink)
    Peptidoglycan Layer Thick Thin
    Outer Membrane Absent Present (additional layer)
    Cell Envelope Glycocalyx + thick peptidoglycan + plasma membrane Glycocalyx + outer membrane + thin peptidoglycan + plasma membrane

    Memory Tip: "Positive People are Thick-skinned" (Gram + = thick peptidoglycan)
    "Negative People have Extra layers" (Gram - = extra outer membrane)

    3. Plasma Membrane (Innermost Layer)

    • Structure: Similar to eukaryotic membrane (phospholipid bilayer)
    • Function: Selectively permeable; controls transport in/out of cell

    B. Mesosome (Unique Prokaryotic Structure)

    • Structure: Infoldings/extensions of plasma membrane into the cytoplasm
    • Forms: Vesicles, tubules, and lamellae
    • Functions:
      1. ATP synthesis (cellular respiration) - PRIMARY FUNCTION
      2. DNA replication assistance
      3. Cell wall formation
      4. Secretion processes
      5. Increases surface area of plasma membrane and enzymatic content

    Why Mesosomes: Prokaryotes lack mitochondria, so mesosomes provide increased membrane surface area for ATP synthase enzymes.

    C. Chromatophores (In Photosynthetic Bacteria)

    • Found in: Cyanobacteria (blue-green algae)
    • Structure: Membranous extensions/infoldings of plasma membrane
    • Content: Photosynthetic pigments
    • Function: Photosynthesis (replaces chloroplast function in prokaryotes)

    Why Chromatophores: Since prokaryotes lack membrane-bound chloroplasts, plasma membrane infoldings contain photosynthetic pigments and increase surface area for pigment accommodation.

    D. Flagella, Pili, and Fimbriae (Surface Structures)

    Flagella (Prokaryotic):

    • Structure: Three parts
      1. Basal body: Anchored in plasma membrane
      2. Hook: Flexible connecting region
      3. Filament: Longest portion extending outside
    • Composition: Flagellin protein
    • Function: Locomotion (only in motile bacteria)
    • Number: Varies (one to many; various arrangements)

    Pili:

    • Structure: Elongated tubular structures
    • Composition: Pilin protein
    • Function: Conjugation (bacterial sexual reproduction)
      • Forms channel between bacteria
      • Genetic material (DNA) transfers through pilus

    Fimbriae:

    • Structure: Short, numerous bristle-like fibers
    • Function: Attachment
      • To other bacteria (colony formation)
      • To host tissues (if parasitic)
      • To rocks/surfaces in streams

    E. Ribosomes (Prokaryotic)

    • Type: 70S (50S large subunit + 30S small subunit)
    • Composition: Ribosomal RNA (rRNA) + proteins
    • Location: Free-floating in cytoplasm (associated with plasma membrane)
    • Function: Protein synthesis
    • Polysome: Multiple ribosomes on one mRNA strand for efficient protein production

    Important: "S" stands for Svedberg Unit, a sedimentation coefficient measuring density and size during centrifugation. S values are NOT additive (50S + 30S ‚â† 80S, but = 70S because combined density changes).

    Memory Tip: Prokaryotic = "7-Eleven is 24/7" (always open) - 70S ribosomes

    F. Inclusion Bodies

    • Structure: Reserve material storage granules (NOT membrane-bound)
    • Location: Free in cytoplasm
    • Types:
      • Phosphate granules
      • Cyanophycean granules
      • Glycogen granules
    • Function: Store reserve food materials

    G. Gas Vacuoles

    • Found in: Aquatic photosynthetic bacteria
    • Structure: Not membrane-bound
    • Function: Provide buoyancy - help bacteria float to access sunlight for photosynthesis

    H. Genetic Material

    • Genomic DNA: Single circular chromosome in nucleoid region (not membrane-bound)
    • Plasmids: Small circular DNA outside genomic DNA
      • Confer unique characteristics (e.g., antibiotic resistance)
      • Used in genetic engineering for bacterial transformation

    6. Eukaryotic Cells

    6.1 General Characteristics

    Eukaryotes include all protists, plants, animals, and fungi.

    Key Features:

    1. Well-defined nucleus: Enclosed by nuclear membrane (nuclear envelope)
    2. Membrane-bound organelles: ER, Golgi, lysosomes, mitochondria, chloroplasts (in plants), vacuoles
    3. Larger size: Generally 10-100 Œºm (larger than prokaryotes)
    4. 80S ribosomes: In cytoplasm; 70S in mitochondria and chloroplasts
    5. Complex locomotory structures: Cilia, flagella with 9+2 arrangement
    6. Cytoskeleton: Network of protein filaments
    7. Genetic material: Organized into linear chromosomes
    8. Cell division: Mitosis and meiosis

    6.2 Plant Cell vs Animal Cell

    Common Features (Both have):

    • Plasma membrane
    • Nucleus with nuclear envelope
    • Cytoplasm
    • Mitochondria
    • Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER)
    • Golgi apparatus
    • Ribosomes (80S in cytoplasm)

    Differences:

    Feature Plant Cell Animal Cell
    Cell Wall Present (cellulose) Absent
    Chloroplasts Present Absent
    Other Plastids Present (chromoplasts, leucoplasts) Absent
    Vacuole Large central vacuole (up to 90% of cell volume) Small or absent
    Centrioles Absent (except in lower plants) Present
    Shape Fixed (due to cell wall) Variable
    Plasmodesmata Present (cytoplasmic connections) Absent
    Lysosomes Rare Common
    Nucleus Position Peripheral (pushed by vacuole) Central
    Energy Storage Starch Glycogen

    7. Cell Membrane (Plasma Membrane)

    7.1 Structure

    Discovery: Detailed structure studied after electron microscope invention (1950s)

    Composition (Human RBC example):

    • Proteins: ~52%
    • Lipids: ~40%
    • Carbohydrates: Small amounts (as glycolipids and glycoproteins)

    Phospholipid Bilayer:

    Structure of Phospholipid:

    • Hydrophilic head: Phosphate group (water-loving) - faces outward
    • Hydrophobic tails: Two fatty acid chains (water-fearing) - face inward

    Why Bilayer?

    • Water present outside cell
    • Water present inside cell (cytoplasm)
    • Hydrophobic tails hide in middle
    • Hydrophilic heads face both aqueous environments

    Other Components:

    • Cholesterol: Provides stability and fluidity
    • Integral Proteins: Embedded fully or partially in membrane
    • Peripheral Proteins: Lie on surface
    • Glycolipids: Carbohydrate + lipid
    • Glycoproteins: Carbohydrate + protein

    7.2 Fluid Mosaic Model

    Proposed by: Singer and Nicolson (1972)

    Key Concepts:

    1. Quasi-fluid: Lipid bilayer has oil-like consistency
    2. Mosaic: Proteins scattered like patches in lipid sea
    3. Lateral movement: Lipids and proteins can move laterally within membrane
    4. Fluidity: Ability of molecules to move - measured as membrane fluidity

    Importance of Fluidity:

    • Cell growth
    • Formation of intercellular junctions
    • Secretion
    • Endocytosis
    • Cell division

    7.4 Transport Mechanisms

    A. Passive Transport (No ATP required)

    Movement along concentration gradient (high ‚Üí low)

    1. Simple Diffusion:

    • Non-polar molecules pass directly through lipid bilayer
    • Examples: O‚ÇÇ, CO‚ÇÇ, lipid-soluble molecules
    • Along concentration gradient

    2. Osmosis:

    • Water diffusion through membrane
    • Through aquaporins (water channels)
    • From higher water concentration ‚Üí lower water concentration
    • Important for cell turgor

    3. Facilitated Diffusion:

    • Polar molecules need carrier proteins
    • Through protein channels/carriers
    • Along concentration gradient
    • Examples: Glucose, amino acids

    B. Active Transport (ATP required)

    Movement against concentration gradient (low ‚Üí high)

    Example: Sodium-Potassium Pump (Na‚Å∫/K‚Å∫ Pump):

    • 3 Na‚Å∫ ions pumped OUT of cell
    • 2 K‚Å∫ ions pumped INTO cell
    • Uses 1 ATP molecule
    • Maintains concentration gradients

    Memory Tip - Passive = "PAL don't need money":
    "PAL" = Passive, ATP-Less (Simple diffusion, Osmosis, Facilitated diffusion)
    Active = "A Tap" (needs ATP)

    8. Cell Wall

    Found in: Plants, fungi, algae, bacteria
    Absent in: Animals

    8.1 Composition (by organism)

    Organism Cell Wall Composition
    Bacteria Peptidoglycan
    Algae Cellulose, galactans, mannans, calcium carbonate
    Higher Plants Cellulose, hemicellulose, pectins, proteins
    Fungi Chitin

    8.2 Structure (Plant Cells)

    Three Layers (Outside to Inside):

    1. Middle Lamella (Outermost):

    • Composition: Calcium pectate
    • Formation: First layer formed during cell division (from cell plate)
    • Function: Cements neighboring cells together
    • Location: Between two adjacent cells

    Common Mistake: Middle lamella is called "middle" because it formed in the MIDDLE of two dividing cells, NOT because of its final position. It's actually the OUTERMOST layer.

    2. Primary Wall:

    • Composition: Cellulose, hemicellulose, pectin
    • Formation: Forms as cell grows (inside middle lamella)
    • Present in: All plant cells
    • Characteristic: Capable of growth
    • Thickness: Variable by cell type

    3. Secondary Wall (in some cells only):

    • Composition: May contain lignin (in sclerenchyma)
    • Formation: After cell stops growing (inside primary wall)
    • Present in: Specialized cells only (not all)
    • Characteristic: Cannot grow; provides rigidity
    • Examples: Sclerenchyma, tracheids

    Key Concept: New cell wall layers can ONLY form toward the cytoplasm (inside), not outside middle lamella, because synthesis occurs in cytoplasm.

    Plasmodesmata:

    • Cytoplasmic connections between neighboring plant cells
    • Pass through cell wall
    • Allow cell-to-cell communication and transport

    8.3 Functions

    1. Shape: Gives definite shape to cell
    2. Protection: Protects from mechanical damage and infection
    3. Structural Support: Prevents bursting or collapsing
    4. Cell-to-cell Interaction: Facilitates communication
    5. Barrier: To undesirable macromolecules

    9. Endomembrane System

    Definition: Coordinated system of membrane-bound organelles whose functions are interrelated.

    Components:

    1. Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER)
    2. Golgi Apparatus
    3. Lysosomes
    4. Vacuoles

    NOT Included (functions not coordinated with above):

    • Mitochondria
    • Chloroplasts
    • Peroxisomes

    Memory Tip - "ERGLV":
    "Every Really Good Learner Visits"
    Endoplasmic Reticulum, Golgi, Lysosomes, Vacuoles

    9.1 Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER)

    Structure:

    • Network of tubular/cisternae structures in cytoplasm
    • Continuous with nuclear envelope
    • Divides cell into two compartments:
      • Luminal (inside ER)
      • Extra-luminal (cytoplasm)

    Two Types:

    A. Rough ER (RER):

    • Appearance: Studded with ribosomes on outer surface
    • Location: Abundant in cells actively synthesizing proteins
    • Continuous with: Outer nuclear membrane
    • Function:
      • Protein synthesis: Provides surface for ribosomes
      • Protein modification and transport

    B. Smooth ER (SER):

    • Appearance: No ribosomes; smooth surface
    • Location: Abundant in cells synthesizing steroid hormones
    • Function:
      • Lipid synthesis: Major site
      • Steroid hormone synthesis: In animal cells
      • Detoxification: In liver cells

    9.2 Golgi Apparatus

    Discovered by: Camillo Golgi (1898)

    Structure:

    • Flattened, disc-shaped sacs called cisternae
    • Stacked parallel to each other
    • Diameter: 0.5-1.0 Œºm
    • Number of cisternae: Varies

    Two Faces:

    • Cis face (Forming face): Convex side toward nucleus and ER - receives vesicles
    • Trans face (Maturing face): Concave side away from nucleus - releases products

    Functions:

    1. Packaging: Materials packaged in vesicles
    2. Modification:
      • Glycosylation: Adding sugars to proteins and lipids
      • Forms glycoproteins and glycolipids
    3. Secretion: Vesicles transport materials to plasma membrane or outside cell
    4. Lysosome Formation: Packages hydrolytic enzymes

    Process Flow:

    1. ER synthesizes proteins
    2. ER pinches off vesicle containing proteins
    3. Vesicle fuses with cis face of Golgi
    4. Proteins modified as they move through cisternae
    5. Modified proteins exit from trans face in vesicles
    6. Vesicles go to destination (membrane, secretion, or become lysosomes)

    Why Close Association with ER: Golgi receives materials from ER, so it remains close for efficient vesicle transport.

    9.3 Lysosomes

    Structure:

    • Membrane-bound vesicles
    • Formed by Golgi apparatus
    • Single membrane

    Contents:

    • Hydrolytic enzymes (hydrolases):
      • Lipases: Break down lipids
      • Proteases: Break down proteins
      • Carbohydrases: Break down carbohydrates
      • Nucleases: Break down nucleic acids
    • Optimally active at acidic pH (~2)

    Functions:

    1. Intracellular Digestion: Digest macromolecules
    2. Autophagy: Digest damaged organelles
    3. Autolysis: Self-destruction of cell
    4. Food Vacuole Digestion: In protists like Amoeba

    Why Called "Suicide Bags":

    • Contain enzymes that can destroy all cellular components
    • Can cause cell death if membrane ruptures
    • Enzymes activated only at acidic pH (safety mechanism)

    Mechanism:

    • Protons (H‚Å∫) pumped into lysosome to lower pH to ~2
    • Only at this acidic pH are enzymes fully active
    • Normal cytoplasm pH (~7) keeps enzymes inactive if leakage occurs

    9.4 Vacuoles

    Structure:

    • Membrane-bound sac
    • Membrane called Tonoplast
    • Single membrane

    Plant Cell Vacuoles:

    • Size: Can occupy up to 90% of cell volume
    • Contents: Water, sap, ions, waste materials
    • Ion concentration: Higher than cytoplasm (active transport)
    • Functions:
      • Storage (water, nutrients, waste)
      • Maintains turgor pressure
      • Transport against concentration gradient
      • Provides rigidity to cell

    Animal Cell Vacuoles:

    1. Contractile Vacuole (in Amoeba):

    • Function: Osmoregulation and excretion
    • Mechanism:
      • Collects excess water (endosmosis)
      • Expels water out of cell
    • Necessity: Prevents cell bursting in freshwater (no cell wall in Amoeba)

    2. Food Vacuole (in Amoeba):

    • Function: Digestion
    • Formation: When food is engulfed (phagocytosis)
    • Process: Fuses with lysosome for digestion

    Q3: Why are lysosomes called "suicide bags"?

    Answer: Lysosomes contain hydrolytic enzymes capable of digesting all cellular components. If the lysosomal membrane ruptures, enzymes are released and can destroy the cell - hence "suicide bags."

    Safeguard: Enzymes are active only at acidic pH (~2), while cytoplasm is neutral (pH ~7).

    Why it matters: The "suicide bag" concept is frequently tested. Understanding the pH mechanism shows deeper knowledge.

    • Shape: Sausage-shaped or cylindrical
    • Size:
      • Diameter: 0.2-1.0 Œºm (average 0.5 Œºm)
      • Length: 1.0-4.1 Œºm

    Double Membrane Structure:

    1. Outer Membrane:

    • Smooth
    • Permeable to small molecules

    2. Inner Membrane:

    • Highly folded into cristae (finger-like projections)

    • Site of electron transport chain

    Compartments:

    1. Intermembrane Space (Perimito chondrial space):

    • Between outer and inner membranes

    2. Matrix (inside inner membrane):

    • Dense, homogeneous substance

    • Has own 70S ribosomes (like bacteria)

    12. Ribosomes

    • Named by: Flemming (chroma = color; stains with basic dyes)

    1. Controls Cell Activities: Regulates organelle functions
    2. Heredity: Contains genetic material (DNA)
    3. Gene Expression: Controls protein synthesis
    4. Stores DNA: Protects genetic information

    • Most cells: One nucleus
    • Multinucleate: Some cells have multiple nuclei
    • Enucleated: Some mature cells lack nucleus (RBCs in mammals, sieve tube cells)

    Overview

    The chapter "Cell: The Unit of Life" is foundational to understanding biology. It covers the basic unit of life - the cell - exploring its discovery, structure, types, and various components that enable life processes.

    Key Concepts at a Glance

    1. Cell Discovery and Theory (1665-1855)

    • 1665: Robert Hooke discovers dead cork cells
    • 1674: Leeuwenhoek observes living cells (bacteria, protozoa)
    • 1831: Robert Brown discovers nucleus
    • 1838-1839: Schleiden and Schwann formulate Cell Theory
    • 1855: Virchow states "all cells from pre-existing cells"

    Cell Theory:

    1. All living organisms are composed of cells and cell products
    2. All cells arise from pre-existing cells

    2. Two Types of Cells

    Feature Prokaryotic Eukaryotic
    Size 1-10 ¬µm 10-100 ¬µm
    Nucleus No true nucleus (nucleoid) Well-defined nucleus
    Organelles No membrane-bound organelles Membrane-bound organelles present
    Ribosomes 70S 80S (70S in organelles)
    DNA Circular, in cytoplasm Linear, in nucleus
    Cell Division Binary fission Mitosis/Meiosis
    Examples Bacteria, Cyanobacteria Plants, Animals, Fungi, Protists

    3. Cell Membrane (Plasma Membrane)

    • Structure: Lipid bilayer (phospholipids) with embedded proteins
    • Model: Fluid Mosaic Model (Singer & Nicolson, 1972)
    • Nature: Quasi-fluid (lipids move laterally)
    • Proteins: Integral (embedded) and Peripheral (surface)
    • Function: Selective permeability, transport, cell recognition

    4. Cell Wall (Plants only)

    Three Layers:

    1. Middle Lamella: Calcium pectate, holds cells together
    2. Primary Wall: Cellulose, thin and flexible
    3. Secondary Wall: Lignin, thick and rigid

    Plasmodesmata: Cytoplasmic bridges between cells

    5. Cell Organelles - Functions Summary

    Organelle Main Function Key Feature
    Nucleus Genetic control, heredity Double membrane, pores, chromatin
    Mitochondria ATP production (respiration) Double membrane, cristae, 70S ribosomes
    Chloroplast Photosynthesis Double membrane, thylakoids/grana, 70S ribosomes
    Endoplasmic Reticulum RER: Protein synthesis; SER: Lipid synthesis Membrane network, attached/free ribosomes
    Golgi Apparatus Modification, packaging, secretion Stacked cisternae, cis and trans face
    Lysosomes Intracellular digestion Single membrane, hydrolytic enzymes
    Vacuoles Storage, turgor pressure Single membrane, large in plants
    Ribosomes Protein synthesis Non-membrane, 80S or 70S
    Centrosome Spindle fiber formation Two centrioles (9+0 arrangement)
    Cilia/Flagella Movement 9+2 microtubule arrangement
    Peroxisomes Fatty acid oxidation, H‚ÇÇO‚ÇÇ breakdown Single membrane, catalase enzyme

    6. Endomembrane System

    Components: ER ‚Üí Golgi ‚Üí Lysosomes/Vacuoles

    Function: Coordinated system for protein synthesis, modification, packaging, and secretion

    Note: Mitochondria and chloroplasts are NOT part of endomembrane system (they're semi-autonomous)

    7. Semi-Autonomous Organelles

    Mitochondria and Chloroplast are called semi-autonomous because:

    • Have their own circular DNA
    • Have 70S ribosomes (prokaryotic type)
    • Can make some of their own proteins
    • Divide independently
    • Endosymbiotic Theory: Evolved from free-living prokaryotes

    8. Plant vs Animal Cells

    Plant Cell ONLY:

    • Cell wall (cellulose)
    • Chloroplasts
    • Large central vacuole
    • Plasmodesmata

    Animal Cell ONLY:

    • Centrioles
    • Small/no vacuoles
    • Lysosomes (common)

    Both Have:

    • Nucleus, mitochondria, ER, Golgi, ribosomes, cytoskeleton

    9. Chromosome Types (Based on Centromere Position)

    1. Metacentric: Centromere in middle (L-shaped)
    2. Sub-metacentric: Centromere slightly off-center (J-shaped)
    3. Acrocentric: Centromere near end (rod-shaped)
    4. Telocentric: Centromere at terminal end (i-shaped)

    üìñ GLOSSARY OF IMPORTANT TERMS

    A

    Acrocentric Chromosome: A chromosome with the centromere located close to one end, resulting in one very short arm and one long arm.

    Aleuroplast: A type of leucoplast that stores proteins.

    Amyloplast: A type of leucoplast that stores starch.

    Autophagy: The process by which lysosomes digest damaged or worn-out cell organelles.

    Autolysis: Self-digestion of cells by their own enzymes, especially after death.

    C

    Cell: The fundamental structural and functional unit of all living organisms.

    Cell Theory: The theory stating that (1) all living organisms are composed of cells and cell products, and (2) all cells arise from pre-existing cells.

    Cell Wall: A rigid outer layer found in plant cells, composed of cellulose, that provides structural support and protection.

    Centriole: A cylindrical organelle composed of nine triplets of microtubules (9+0 arrangement), found in animal cells, involved in cell division.

    Centromere: The primary constriction on a chromosome where sister chromatids are joined and where spindle fibers attach during cell division.

    Centrosome: The organelle that serves as the main microtubule organizing center, contains two centrioles.

    Chloroplast: A double membrane-bound organelle in plant cells where photosynthesis occurs, contains chlorophyll.

    Chromatin: A complex of DNA and histone proteins found in the nucleus during interphase, which condenses to form chromosomes during cell division.

    Chromoplast: Colored plastids containing carotenoid pigments (red, yellow, orange), found in flowers and fruits.

    Chromosome: A highly condensed form of chromatin visible during cell division, consisting of DNA and associated proteins.

    Cilia: Short, hair-like projections on the cell surface with 9+2 microtubule arrangement, used for movement or moving substances across the cell surface.

    Cisternae: Flattened, membrane-bound sacs that make up the Golgi apparatus and endoplasmic reticulum.

    Cristae: The infoldings of the inner mitochondrial membrane that increase surface area for ATP synthesis.

    Cytoplasm: The gel-like substance inside the cell membrane, excluding the nucleus, containing organelles and dissolved substances.

    Cytoskeleton: A network of protein filaments (microfilaments, intermediate filaments, microtubules) that provides structural support and enables cell movement.

    E

    Elaioplast: A type of leucoplast that stores oils and fats.

    Endomembrane System: A system of membrane-bound organelles (ER, Golgi, lysosomes, vacuoles) that work together in synthesis, modification, and transport.

    Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER): A network of membranous tubules and sacs involved in protein and lipid synthesis.

    Endosymbiotic Theory: The theory that mitochondria and chloroplasts originated as free-living prokaryotic organisms that were engulfed by ancestral eukaryotic cells.

    Eukaryotic Cell: A cell with a well-defined, membrane-bound nucleus and membrane-bound organelles.

    F

    Fimbriae: Short, hair-like structures on bacterial surfaces used for attachment to surfaces.

    Flagella: Long, whip-like projections with 9+2 microtubule arrangement, used for cell movement.

    Fluid Mosaic Model: The model of cell membrane structure (Singer & Nicolson, 1972) describing a fluid lipid bilayer with proteins embedded in a mosaic pattern.

    G

    Golgi Apparatus: A stack of flattened membrane-bound sacs (cisternae) involved in modifying, packaging, and sorting proteins and lipids.

    Grana: Stacks of thylakoids in chloroplasts where light reactions of photosynthesis occur (singular: granum).

    H

    Hydrolytic Enzymes: Enzymes that break down substances by adding water molecules, abundant in lysosomes.

    I

    Inclusion Bodies: Non-living structures in prokaryotic cells that store reserve materials like glycogen, lipids, or gas vesicles.

    Integral Proteins: Membrane proteins that are embedded within the lipid bilayer.

    L

    Leucoplast: Colorless plastids found in non-photosynthetic plant tissues, used for storage.

    Lysosome: A membrane-bound organelle containing hydrolytic enzymes for intracellular digestion, called "suicidal bags" of the cell.

    M

    Matrix: The fluid-filled inner space of mitochondria containing enzymes for the Krebs cycle.

    Metacentric Chromosome: A chromosome with the centromere in the middle, creating two equal arms (L-shaped).

    Mesosome: An infolding of the plasma membrane in prokaryotic cells, involved in respiration and cell wall formation.

    Microbodies: Small, membrane-bound organelles containing enzymes (peroxisomes, glyoxysomes).

    Middle Lamella: The outermost layer of plant cell wall made of calcium pectate that cements adjacent cells together.

    Mitochondria: Double membrane-bound organelles known as the "powerhouse of the cell," responsible for ATP production through cellular respiration.

    N

    Nuclear Envelope: The double membrane surrounding the nucleus, containing nuclear pores.

    Nuclear Pores: Openings in the nuclear envelope that allow selective movement of molecules between nucleus and cytoplasm.

    Nucleoid: The region in prokaryotic cells where the circular DNA is located, not surrounded by a membrane.

    Nucleolus: A dense, non-membrane-bound structure within the nucleus where ribosomal RNA is synthesized.

    Nucleoplasm: The gel-like substance inside the nucleus, also called nuclear matrix or karyolymph.

    Nucleus: The membrane-bound organelle in eukaryotic cells that contains genetic material (DNA) and controls cellular activities.

    O

    Organelle: A specialized sub-cellular structure with a specific function.

    Oxysomes (F‚ÇÅ particles): ATP synthase enzymes located on the cristae of mitochondria, also called elementary particles.

    P

    Peripheral Proteins: Membrane proteins loosely attached to the surface of the membrane, not embedded in the lipid bilayer.

    Peroxisome: A microbody containing enzymes for fatty acid oxidation and hydrogen peroxide breakdown (catalase enzyme).

    Pili: Hollow, tubular structures on bacterial surfaces used for DNA transfer during conjugation.

    Plasma Membrane: The selectively permeable membrane that surrounds the cell, composed of a lipid bilayer with embedded proteins.

    Plasmodesmata: Cytoplasmic channels that connect adjacent plant cells through their cell walls.

    Plasmid: Small, circular, extra-chromosomal DNA found in prokaryotic cells.

    Plastids: Colored or colorless organelles found in plant cells (chloroplasts, chromoplasts, leucoplasts).

    Primary Cell Wall: The first layer of cell wall formed in plant cells, thin and flexible, composed of cellulose and pectin.

    Prokaryotic Cell: A cell that lacks a true nucleus and membrane-bound organelles, with DNA in a nucleoid region.

    Protoplasm: The living substance of a cell, including cytoplasm and nucleoplasm.

    Q

    Quasi-fluid: The semi-fluid nature of the cell membrane lipid bilayer, where lipid molecules can move laterally.

    R

    Ribosomes: Non-membrane-bound organelles composed of RNA and proteins, responsible for protein synthesis (70S in prokaryotes, 80S in eukaryotes).

    Rough Endoplasmic Reticulum (RER): ER with ribosomes attached, involved in protein synthesis and transport.

    S

    Satellite (SAT) Chromosome: A chromosome with a secondary constriction creating a small segment beyond it, called a satellite.

    Secondary Cell Wall: The thick, rigid layer deposited inside the primary wall in mature plant cells, contains lignin.

    Semi-autonomous Organelles: Organelles (mitochondria, chloroplasts) that have their own DNA and ribosomes and can replicate independently.

    Smooth Endoplasmic Reticulum (SER): ER without ribosomes, involved in lipid synthesis and detoxification.

    Stroma: The fluid-filled space inside chloroplasts where dark reactions (Calvin cycle) of photosynthesis occur.

    Sub-metacentric Chromosome: A chromosome with the centromere slightly off-center, creating one shorter and one longer arm (J-shaped).

    T

    Telocentric Chromosome: A chromosome with the centromere at the terminal end, creating a rod-shaped (i-shaped) structure.

    Thylakoid: Flattened membranous sacs in chloroplasts where light reactions of photosynthesis occur.

    V

    Vacuole: A membrane-bound sac used for storage; large central vacuole in plant cells maintains turgor pressure.
