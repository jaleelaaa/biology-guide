Q1: Who discovered the cell and when?
Answer: Robert Hooke discovered the cell in 1665 while observing a thin slice of cork under a microscope. He saw box-like structures and called them "cells" (from Latin cellula meaning small rooms).

Why it matters: This is a basic fact question frequently asked in both Kerala Board and NEET exams. Remember: Robert Hooke (1665) + Cork.

Note: What Hooke actually saw were dead plant cell walls, not living cells.
Q2: Who first observed living cells?
Answer: Anton van Leeuwenhoek first observed living cells under a microscope. He observed protozoans which he called "animalcules".

Why it matters: This distinguishes between Hooke (who saw dead cells) and Leeuwenhoek (who saw living cells).
Q3: State the three postulates of Cell Theory and name the scientists
Three Postulates:
  1. All living organisms are composed of cells and products of cells
  2. The cell is the basic unit of life
  3. All cells arise from pre-existing cells

Scientists:
  • Matthias Schleiden (1838) - Studied plants, concluded plant tissues are made of cells
  • Theodor Schwann (1839) - Extended idea to animals, formulated cell theory with Schleiden
  • Rudolf Virchow (1855) - Added third postulate about cell division

Why it matters: Cell theory questions appear almost every year in exams. Knowing all three postulates with scientists' names is essential for full marks.
Q4: Who discovered the nucleus and when?
Answer: Robert Brown discovered the nucleus in 1831. He was the first person to describe the nucleus as a cell organelle.

Why it matters: Often confused with Robert Hooke. Remember: Hooke = Cell (1665), Brown = Nucleus (1831).
Q5: What is the Fluid Mosaic Model?
Answer: The Fluid Mosaic Model was proposed by Singer and Nicolson in 1972 to explain the structure of cell membrane.

Key Points:
  • Fluid: Phospholipids and proteins can move laterally within the membrane
  • Mosaic: Proteins embedded in lipid bilayer create a mosaic pattern
  • Quasi-fluid nature allows flexibility and self-sealing

Why it matters: Very high-weightage topic for NEET. Almost guaranteed 1 question every year.
Q6: What are semi-autonomous organelles and why?
Answer: Mitochondria and Chloroplasts are called semi-autonomous organelles.

Reasons:
  1. They have their own DNA (circular, like bacteria)
  2. They have their own 70S ribosomes
  3. They can make some of their own proteins
  4. They divide independently by binary fission
  5. Double membrane structure

Why it matters: NEET favorite topic. Supports endosymbiotic theory.
Q7: Why is Mycoplasma an exception among prokaryotes?
Answer: Mycoplasma is a prokaryote but lacks a cell wall, making it wall-less. It is the smallest prokaryotic cell (0.3 Œºm).

Why it matters: Mycoplasma appears in "exception" type questions in both Kerala Board and NEET exams.
Q8: What are pili and what is their function?
Answer: Pili are elongated tubular structures made of pilin protein on bacterial surface.

Function: Conjugation - bacterial sexual reproduction. Pilus forms a channel through which genetic material (DNA) is transferred from one bacterium to another.

Why it matters: Often confused with flagella. Remember: Pili = conjugation (sex); Flagella = locomotion (movement).

Why it matters: Gram staining is an important classification tool. Remember: "Thick" goes with "Positive".
Q10: Who proposed the Fluid Mosaic Model and when?
Answer: Singer and Nicolson proposed the Fluid Mosaic Model in 1972.

Why it matters: This is frequently asked in both Kerala Board (2 marks) and NEET MCQs. Remember the year 1972.
Q11: What is the composition of cell membrane?
Answer:
  • Lipids (~40%): Mainly phospholipids, also cholesterol
  • Proteins (~52%): Integral and peripheral proteins
  • Carbohydrates (small amount): As glycolipids and glycoproteins

Why it matters: Composition questions appear in exams. Remember proteins > lipids in amount.

Q13: What are the components of the endomembrane system?
Answer: Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER), Golgi apparatus, Lysosomes, and Vacuoles.

Why it matters: Endomembrane system questions test understanding of how organelles work together. Remember the ERGLV mnemonic.
Q14: Differentiate between Rough ER and Smooth ER
Feature Rough ER Smooth ER
Ribosomes Present on surface Absent
Function Protein synthesis Lipid synthesis
Location Abundant in protein-secreting cells Abundant in steroid-producing cells

Why it matters: RER vs SER is a standard 2-3 mark question in Kerala Board and NEET MCQ.
Q15: Why are lysosomes called "suicide bags"?
Answer: Lysosomes contain hydrolytic enzymes capable of digesting all cellular components. If the lysosomal membrane ruptures, enzymes are released and can destroy the cell - hence "suicide bags."

Safeguard: Enzymes are active only at acidic pH (~5), while cytoplasm is neutral (pH ~7).

Why it matters: The "suicide bag" concept is frequently tested. Understanding the pH mechanism shows deeper knowledge.
Q16: What is glycosylation and where does it occur?
Answer:
Glycosylation: Process of adding sugar molecules to proteins or lipids.

Where: Golgi apparatus

Products: Glycoproteins (protein + carbohydrate) and Glycolipids (lipid + carbohydrate)

Why it matters: Golgi function questions often ask about glycosylation. This is a key modification process.
Q17: Why is mitochondria called the "powerhouse of the cell"?
Answer: Mitochondria is called the powerhouse because it produces ATP (adenosine triphosphate), the cell's energy currency, through cellular respiration (oxidative phosphorylation).

Why it matters: This is THE most frequently asked fact about mitochondria in both board exams and NEET. Always mention ATP when answering.
Q18: Why do mitochondria and chloroplasts have their own DNA and ribosomes?
Answer: Both are semi-autonomous organelles:
  • Have their own circular DNA (like bacteria)
  • Have their own 70S ribosomes (like prokaryotes)
  • Can synthesize some of their own proteins
  • Divide by fission

This supports the endosymbiotic theory - they were once free-living bacteria engulfed by primitive eukaryotic cells.

Why it matters: This concept connects to evolution and appears in reasoning-based NEET questions.
Q19: What is the difference between chloroplasts and mitochondria?
Feature Chloroplast Mitochondria
Function Photosynthesis (energy capture) Cellular respiration (energy release)
Found in Plants, algae All eukaryotes
Pigments Chlorophyll, carotenoids None
Internal membranes Thylakoids, grana Cristae

Both have: Circular DNA, 70S ribosomes (semi-autonomous)


Why it matters: Comparison questions are common. Both are semi-autonomous despite different functions.
Q20: Name three types of plastids with their functions
Answer:
  1. Chloroplasts: Green; contain chlorophyll; perform photosynthesis
  2. Chromoplasts: Yellow/orange/red; contain carotenoids; color to flowers/fruits
  3. Leucoplasts: Colorless; store nutrients
    • Amyloplast: stores starch
    • Elaioplast: stores oils/fats
    • Aleuroplast: stores proteins

Why it matters: Plastid classification is a standard 3-mark Kerala Board question.
